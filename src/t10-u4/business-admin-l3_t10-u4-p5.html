Understand the market

Price elasticity of demand

Price elasticity of demand (PED) is a measure of the extent to which demand for goods or services is responsive to price changes. Price elasticity of demand depends on how essential the good or service is. Is it viewed as a luxury or necessity?

  • Luxury goods = elastic.
  • Essential = inelastic.

The law of demand states that as price rises, demand falls. Therefore, PED will always have a negative value.

Factors affecting PED
  • Necessity of the good/service — the more essential it is, the lower its elasticity.
  • Number of available substitutes — the more substitutes available, the higher its elasticity.
  • Addictiveness — habit forming goods, such as alcohol, can be highly inelastic.
  • Cost and usefulness — the smaller the proportion of income that is spent on cheap/useful goods, the more inelastic they will be.
  • Time period — in the short run, demand is more inelastic; in the long run, it tends to be more elastic.
  • Consumer awareness — more information leads to higher elasticity.
Supply and demand

For most goods and services the relationship between demand and price is inverse – when price is high, demand is low; when price is low, demand is high. Demand is also affected by a range of other factors such as disposable income and advertising.

For most goods and services, the relationship between supply and price is positive – when price is low, demand is low; when price is high, demand is high and vice versa. Supply is affected by a range of other factors such as technological development and taxes/subsidies.
