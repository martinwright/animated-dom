Organisational culture and strategy

2 Why these objectives need to be SMART

Many organisations use SMART targets as they provide a logical and simple template of how to set and monitor objectives and activities. The objectives need to be:

  • Specific
    to identify the objective and desired outcome clearly, and to show the action and resources required.
  • Measurable
    so that everyone can see the schedule of work, track progress and measure success.
  • Achievable
    to make sure that the objective is within the scope of the team’s abilities and available resources.
  • Realistic
    to make sure that everything is achievable with the time, working methods and resources that are available.
  • Time-bound
    deadlines that are aligned with the organisational targets give a focus for individuals, the team and the organisation.

Once managers have agreed the objectives with the rest of the organisation, they can involve team leaders and team members in creating SMART objectives or targets that align with achieving the organisational targets. The SMART objectives can focus on the team as a whole or on individual team members.

By involving team members in agreeing and setting objectives, the benefits can include:

  • Improved motivation and empowerment
    Team members feel valued and integral parts of the whole process. By taking responsibility and ownership of the project, they are more likely to engage and enjoy the challenge of achieving the objectives. Assisting in the planning of objectives encourages trust and inspires team members to develop their own personal growth and be part of the achievement culture. It also empowers people by giving them the opportunity to voice concerns and discuss potential problems early on in the process.
  • Improved decision making
    The openness that features when planning objectives together should feature in all elements of the project. Good communication links allow information about progress, problems and changes to flow effectively. In addition, team members are more likely to prioritise their own activities when they can see the overall impact of their decisions.
  • Increased productivity and product quality
    Team members can offer useful suggestions, opinions and creative ideas at the beginning of a project or task that can improve the whole process. Being part of the process, from planning objectives to the final delivery, encourages people to take ownership and deal with problems and changes more quickly and effectively. It is in their interests to be part of success, not failure.
  • Clarity and focus
    When individual team members can see how their objectives tie in with those of the team and the organisation, they have a clearer view of the overall aims and reasons behind decisions. This focuses their attention and gives them direction.

Collaboration when setting objectives helps to ensure the team’s success and achievement of team and organisational goals. When team members have been part of the planning process, they are more engaged and determined to see a satisfactory outcome, which benefits the team members, managers and organisation. The openness about planning and agreeing the objectives can continue, making it easier to focus the team’s attention, keep an eye on problems, and to achieve and measure success.
