Business process improvements

7 Root cause analysis (RCA)

This is a technique that finds possible solutions by identifying the root causes of problems. It is also referred to as cause and effect analysis and makes use of fishbone diagrams, herringbone diagrams and Ishikawa diagrams.

The technique can be used to:

  • discover the root cause of problems, especially for complicated problems
  • reveal ‘bottlenecks’ in a process that causes delays
  • identify where and why a process does not work
  • improve quality control.

There are four steps to using this technique:

  • Step 1
    identify the problem – eg what the problem is, who is involved, where and when it occurs.
  • Step 2
    work out the major factors involved – eg systems or methods, equipment/machinery, materials, management issues, people or environment.
  • Step 3
    identify possible causes of the problem – eg brainstorm the different factors in step 2 and add them to the diagram.
  • Step 4
    analyse the diagram – eg look at all the possible causes and identify problem areas that have a knock-on effect on other issues – work to identify the root causes.

As in our example above, the ‘bones’ can be labelled with any factor relevant to the problem. However the typical factors used when working through a fishbone diagram would be:

  • Materials
    is the problem caused by any of the materials used, eg defective, delivered late, wrong type?
  • Machinery
    is the problem caused by machinery issues, eg unreliable/broken/out of date?
  • Methods
    is the problem caused by working methods, eg out of date processes?
  • Management issues
    is the problem caused by, eg budget restrictions, headcount limits?
  • People
    is the problem being caused by staff, eg those new to the role, or who need more training or support?
  • Environment
    is the problem being caused by the physical environment, eg too hot or cold, heating/lighting, space or location?

Once the problem has been clearly defined and the nature, scope and impact is understood, we can then work through these factors to identify the root cause, or in more complex problems, causes.

There can also be links between the different factors and causes. For example, there may be a process that is out of date and is contributing to the problem. Another cause might be that people are not working effectively because they are following an out of date process. This may mean a solution that both updates the process and trains the relevant people to use the new process.

Where there are a number of issues, it may be because there is more than one definable problem. For example, customers might be calling to complain about a defective product, however the customer relationship management (CRM) system keeps crashing which is resulting in further complaints. Following an investigation it may be found that there is a specific problem with a product which is causing the customer complaints in the first place. However, it may also be that there is a problem with the CRM system. In this case there are two definable problems – firstly with the defective product and secondly with the CRM system.
