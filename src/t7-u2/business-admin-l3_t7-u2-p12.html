Business process improvements

8 Lean production

In his 1990 book, The Machine That Changed the World, James Womack explains how Toyota’s approach to production proved to be a more efficient and successful system than the traditional mass production approaches in use in the west at that time. This approach to production of cars (known as the Toyota Production System) became what is now known as ‘lean’.

The online dictionary defines lean manufacturing as follows:

'Doing more with less by employing "lean thinking". Lean manufacturing involves never ending efforts to eliminate or reduce "muda" (Japanese for waste or any activity that consumes resources without adding value) in design, manufacturing, distribution, and customer service processes …'

It has also been described as: ‘… doing more with less …’ Anderson et al (2013).

Lean production seeks to eliminate waste from the system of manufacturing without compromising quality. This also means providing increased value to the customer by providing a product they want, manufactured without unnecessary waste. A reduced level of waste speeds up production and keeps costs, and therefore the price to the customer, down.

Although initially adopted by manufacturing, there are some key principles in lean that are valuable in all work settings. No organisation wants to incur waste where it does not need to. The basic lean principles we are going to look at are:

  • seven forms of waste
  • just in time
  • zero defects.

Seven forms of waste (muda) – in lean, waste is broadly split into two categories:

  • waste that is not necessary and does not add any value
  • waste that does not add value but cannot be eliminated for some reason.

In the first category, where waste is both unnecessary and does not add any value to either the organisation or the customer, then lean would suggest these are the first and most pressing targets for removal.

The second category of waste is one that does not provide any value but cannot be removed. An example would be where, for legal or regulatory reasons, hard copies of documents have to be printed for audit or inspection. It is a waste to print a document held electronically but the inspection regime requires hard copies.

Lean’s seven forms of waste help managers to focus on the different parts of any operation to identify and potentially remove any unnecessary waste. The seven forms are as follows:

Form of waste:

Description:

Examples:

Transportation
Includes any unnecessary or inefficient movement of materials or products – only move anything if it is necessary and is part of the process
  • Deliveries made to wrong address or office.
  • Storing materials or documents in the wrong place meaning they have to be moved again.
  • Moving materials or equipment using inappropriately sized vehicles/trucks.
Waiting
Waiting or delays for anything necessary to the process is waste. This might be waiting for materials, equipment, people, information etc
  • Waiting for sign-off from line manager.
  • Waiting for any information that stops work continuing.
  • Waiting for corrected errors in any work process.
  • Waiting for printer paper delaying printing of resources.
Overproduction
Making more than is needed and before being needed is waste – this will also cause other wastes such as inventory and transportation
  • Printing or photocopying any document beyond the amount needed.
  • Writing a ten-page overly detailed report where a three-page summary would suffice.
Defects
Including repairs and rejects; defects relate to any work or product that falls short of the required specification – any work not done correct the first time
  • Any data input errors that then need to be corrected.
  • Any missing data that could have been entered first time around.
  • Incorrect invoice or purchase order numbers.
  • Printing onto the wrong paper.
Inventory
Having too much stock or inventory means risking it being damaged, lost, stolen, and using valuable workspace inefficiently
  • Over purchase of office stationary.
  • Printing off more copies of brochures or leaflets that then sit on a shelf.
  • Over purchase of products for sale.
Motion
Includes any unnecessary movement made by human resources; any lifting, stretching, walking or any movement that is not required increases risk of injury and slows down processes
  • Putting needed resources on high shelves.
  • Overfilling filing cabinets or looking for lost files whether hard copy or electronically.
  • Walking further because a copier is broken.
Extra processing
Any process or step in a process or procedure that does not add value to the customer – over working the same product or process or doing a job twice
  • Printing colour copies when blank and white would have been sufficient.
  • Printing one-sided copies when double-sided would have been sufficient.
  • Sending global emails unnecessarily causing others to take time to read them even if not relevant to them.
