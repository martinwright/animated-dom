10 Kaizen

Translated the word 'kai' means 'change' or ‘change over time’ and 'zen' means ‘good’ or 'to make better'. However kaizen tends to be more commonly referred to as continuous improvement.

Masaaki Imai pioneered kaizen in 1980's Japan as a way of improving business processes and quality. Here kaizen is being applied to operational management and specifically improving quality and reducing waste.

Incorporating many of the best tools and techniques related to the growth and development of manufacturing in Japan in the 1980s (JIT/PDCA/fishbone etc), kaizen epitomises continuous, incremental and orderly small changes that create a momentum of quality and business improvements in every part of an organisation.

Japanese companies in the 1980s became so good at this that they have changed the world of manufacturing. So much so that most large manufacturers around the world now adopt kaizen (and lean) as normal working practices.

Like lean, kaizen focuses on reducing and removing waste wherever possible and the seven forms of waste discussed earlier are relevant to kaizen. Just as important, however, is the need to keep and maintain a tidy workspace. Kaizen’s 5S housekeeping approach provides a valuable framework for ‘good housekeeping’ to create the right conditions for kaizen to flourish. The 5Ss are:

1. Seiri – tidiness

Sort what is needed for the work from what is not needed and remove the latter. If it is needed constantly for the work then it stays

2. Seiton – order

A place for everything and everything in its place. This step ensures everyone knows where they can find what they need, when they need it. It also considers how processes flow and whether they are as efficient as possible

3. Seiso – cleanliness

Keeping all workspaces clean and shiny. Everyone should be part of ensuring this happens. Workspaces should be neatly kept and orderly

4. Seiketsu – standardise

Once there is proper tidiness, order and cleanliness, this S seeks to standardise and make the first three normal practice. Once a workspace is ‘set’ it should be maintained that way by all. This can be further embedded through photographs of correctly laid out workspaces, so everyone knows what they should look like

5. Shitsuke – discipline

This is about sustaining the 5Ss and embedding them and kaizen deep into the culture of the workforce and the organisation. It is leading by example so that everyone develops a ‘kaizen mind’ while working to uphold kaizen and levels of housekeeping and create a continuously improving culture

The first three Ss are workforce led and are the practical application of the principles ensuring the removal of waste, establishing order and maintaining clean work spaces. They are also highly visible and observers can see whether areas are tidy, orderly and clean.

The last two Ss are about effective management and leadership ensuring that everyone applies what has been agreed consistently. They also work to embed the approach into the culture or ‘the way things are done’.
