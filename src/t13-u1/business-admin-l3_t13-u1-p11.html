Project lifecycle and roles

7 Project delivery and control

Having spent a great deal of time and effort planning a project, there comes a time when the project team needs to put all their theory and preparation into practice and deliver the project. This can be an exciting and stressful time when everything is tested for real. Decision-making and problem-solving skills are needed to deal with the inevitable queries and problems that could threaten to alter the progress of the project.

To keep things on track, progress needs to be monitored and measured so that any potential problems are identified as soon as possible, maybe against SMART targets, planners, flow charts, budgets and risk assessments.

Monitoring and controlling a project can involve, for example, performing internal and external audits. Larger projects may be audited by internal or external audits, especially if the organisation is a limited company or a charity where there is a legal requirement to monitor activities. Internal audits can be done by someone who works for the organisation – eg a manager. External audits are carried out by people outside the organisation – eg registered accountants or auditors.

Regular progress reviews and team meetings during a project will help to identify actual and potential problems as they arise. By reviewing flow charts, task lists, planners, project plans, objectives and so on, the team can see the problems, and reprioritise and allocate resources to deal with the issues.
