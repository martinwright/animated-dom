Decision-making techniques

1 SWOT analysis

One technique is to perform a SWOT analysis. You can produce these in a simple way to help to clarify your thinking and focus your attention on all aspects of the problem. A SWOT analysis is particularly useful for gathering, interpreting and analysing information. For each realistic possible solution, you can analyse the following:

S – Strengths

W – Weaknesses

O – Opportunities

T – Threats to success

If an important but expensive piece of machinery keeps breaking down in the workplace, the SWOT analysis could show, for example:

Option A – have it fixed every time to keep it going for as long as possible – maybe another year

Option B – replace the machine with a brand new, up to date version that should last twenty years

Option C – replace it with a reconditioned, identical machine that should last five years

Strengths

Not too expensive

The problems are well-known and familiar

Good reliability

Up to date technology

Good warranty support

Reasonable cost

Its service history should indicate reliability

Staff know how to operate it without further training

Weaknesses

Frequent disruption of production that cannot be planned

Loss of efficiency and productivity

Very expensive

Disruptive to install

Staff training time required

Does not benefit from the most up to date technology and design

Only a short warranty

Could develop problems at any time

Opportunities

Easy to arrange with established engineers

Three possible suppliers

Available in about two months

Plenty available if researched online and using trade networks

Could be installed quickly as changes to infrastructure not required

Threats to success

Staff may refuse to work with it when it breaks again

Customers could be lost due to delays

Cost and budget constraints

Might not be needed after a few years

Additional productivity may not justify the cost

Cost might not be justifiable if long-term production plans are for twenty years

Staff and other managers may argue strongly for having a new machine

Although a SWOT analysis does not give a magic answer to the problem, it does help to identify the pros and cons of each option. Once you know the budget constraints and the long-term organisational targets that could be affected by the machine in the example, you would be able to make the best decision to solve the problem.
