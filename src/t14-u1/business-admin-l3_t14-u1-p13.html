Legislation and regulations

Consumer protection and legislation

Organisations have a range of contractual and statutory duties which affect the way products or services can be delivered.

Trade Descriptions Act – This act prohibits organisations from applying a false trade description to any goods. It also covers the supply or offer to supply any goods to which a false trade description has been applied. Organisations are not allowed to make certain kinds of false statement about the provision of any services, facilities or accommodation. This law is enforced by trading standards officers. Although still in force, this Act has largely been replaced by the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations.

Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 – Protects consumers from unfair or misleading trading practices and bans misleading omissions and aggressive sales tactics.

Consumer Protection Act – This act protects the public by prohibiting the manufacture and supply of unsafe goods; making the manufacturer or seller of a defective product responsible for damage it causes; allowing local councils to seize unsafe goods and suspend the sale of suspected unsafe goods; and prohibiting misleading price indications. Customers can claim compensation for death, injury or damage to property over a certain amount if a product they use turns out to be faulty. Producers and distributors of goods are required to ensure that their products are safe and it is an offence to display or to give a customer misleading information.

Consumer Credit Act – This act regulates consumer credit and covers areas such as: eligibility for credit schemes, cooling-off periods, content of credit agreements and calculation of APR.

Misrepresentation Act 1967 – This act exists to protect consumers from false or fraudulent claims that induce you into buying something or entering a contract. The Act allows you to claim damages in the case of fraudulent misrepresentation.

Consumer Contracts Regulations – Give customers rights when shopping online, so they are covered if things go wrong.

Consumer Rights Act – Replaced three major pieces of consumer legislation – the Sale of Goods Act, Unfair Terms in Consumer Contracts Regulations and the Supply of Goods and Services Act. It was introduced to simplify, strengthen and modernise the law, giving customers clearer shopping rights.

The Consumers’ Association is a registered charity and limited company which is better known by its brand name, Which? Its purpose is to encourage consumers to make informed choices about their purchase of services and goods and it does this by testing and comparing similar products, and providing information on inferior products and services. Through campaigns and its website The Consumers’ Association raises awareness of consumers’ rights and provides independent advice.

The British Standards Institution (BSI Group) owns and operates the BSI Kitemark which is a UK product and service quality certification mark. The kitemark identifies products where safety is paramount, such as children's car seats and fire alarms.
