Change in the workplace

4 The change curve

The change curve was developed in the 1960s by Elisabeth Kübler-Ross to explain the grieving process. It has since been developed further to apply to change management, helping to explain people’s reactions to major upheaval and change. It is a useful tool when managing individual or team change as it helps managers to decide what level of support someone needs.

It is based on three stages.

Stage 1 – shock and denial

Shock is often the first reaction to change, and can be caused by lack of information, fear of the unknown, fear of looking stupid or doing something wrong. Shock can lead to decreased performance and people needing more reassurance and guidance.

It can be followed by denial, when people convince themselves that change is not happening or that it will not affect them. They can feel threatened and afraid of failure.

Stage 2 – anger and depression

Anger is often the next stage, and people try to find someone to blame – eg managers or the organisation. Common feelings include suspicion, scepticism or frustration. When anger wears off, it is frequently replaced by depression, which shows itself as apathy, isolation and remoteness.

Stage 3 – acceptance and integration

After the negative and difficult emotions in the first two stages, optimism and enthusiasm start to return in the final stage. People accept that change is inevitable and begin to embrace exciting new opportunities and feel relief that they have survived.

Integration is the final step, when the main feelings are based on acceptance, hope and trust. It is not uncommon to return to the previous stages if the level of support drops suddenly, so good communication is critical to enable the optimism and enthusiasm to continue and develop.

When supporting the team through a period of change, this theory helps managers to understand how the negative emotions in the early stages usually lead to more positive emotions and enthusiasm. This needs to be communicated to team members, so that they understand the process too and do not worry too much about negative reactions.
