Change in the workplace

6 Nudge theory

Nudge theory was made popular in the 2008 book Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth and Happiness by Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein. The theory can be used to explore and explain influences that affect how people behave, and how to remove or alter them using ‘nudges’ such as advertising or government information.

People who advocate nudge theory use indirect methods to influence people, rather than direct enforcement or instruction – eg help and assistance rather than enforcement or policing; enablement and facilitation rather than legislation, rules and laws; education and information rather than instruction and direction.

When supporting teams through change, nudge theory helps managers to remember to make things as positive as possible, and focus on indirect methods of communication. The theory relies on individuals being guided rather than told what to do, so that they find their own way to success and change. Team members benefit from being guided to discover new objectives, plans, knowledge, skills and procedures for themselves. Learning is often simpler and more enjoyable, and they feel engaged in the change and development processes.
