Communicating effectively

The barriers to effective communication

At various points in the communication process there are potential barriers to effective communication. This section is about identifying these barriers and also explaining how they could be overcome.

Here are some key barriers to communication in organisations and some suggested options for overcoming them:

Barrier to communication

Potential causes

Options for overcoming the barriers
Physical/geographical location
  • Where there is a spread of the team(s) or workforce across different locations and/or regions or global territories.
  • Where workforce operates remotely and works at home or spends large amounts of time ‘on the road’.
  • Teams not being in contact regularly and messages being forgotten or lost.
  • International teams working in different time zones means working hours rarely overlap.
  • Schedule and agree regular check-ins as appropriate via telephone, web meeting or call/conference call.
  • Agree regularity of face-to-face meetings and schedule in so that work diaries can be built around them.
  • Be flexible about venues for meetings whether group or one to one and make use of other organisational sites, hotels, cafes as appropriate.
  • Sharing of work diaries, schedules and plans to allow for better planning of communications.
  • Provide meeting notes and attachments electronically to support content of the meeting.
Work culture and environment
  • Physical workspace may lack room for team meetings or have limited space and not always available.
  • Operational work environment means access to IT is intermittent for some workers.
  • Operational work environment means verbal communication is difficult due to noise or appropriate time/space.
  • Busy work culture means people are easily distracted or interrupted.
  • Arrange team meetings away from operational workspace if possible.
  • Have highly regular team ‘huddles’ to discuss issues and challenges and to keep information flowing.
  • Make use of noticeboards and newsletters in kitchens and sitting areas.
  • Take meetings ‘off-site’ where possible to minimise distractions.
  • Provide meeting notes and attachments electronically to support content of the meeting.
  • Agree meeting protocols so that phones/laptops and tablets are off and not on the meeting table.
  • Offer cross-department training or briefing to educate one team about what another team does.
Status of sender and audience
  • People feel intimidated at being addressed by highly senior people in the organisation.
  • Managers asked to deliver presentations to the board of directors for the first time.
  • Senior managers having little contact with the workforce, or making contact at certain times of the year only (eg Christmas).
  • Communications down the chain of command only being done when there is a problem or negative feedback provided.
  • Consider content of the message carefully and how the audience may be feeling about having the boss with them.
  • Consider how to dress for the needs of your audience – might need to ‘dress down’ or ‘dress up’ as needed.
  • Be clear about the protocols that exist for planning and delivering communications to the board.
  • May need to complete some fact finding or research about the audience beforehand.
  • Where there is concern because of status then the more senior person or the person leading should reassure the other(s) of the purpose of the communication and expectations.
Use of language/jargon
  • The use of overly technical language or jargon where not appropriate, such as with new recruits to the organisation who are not yet familiar.
  • The use of technical language suitable in one function but potentially misunderstood or not known in another, eg accounting terms.
  • Consider the audience and whether the message they need requires technical terms and language.
  • Where technical language is to be used, consider explaining key terms before using them.
  • Create glossaries or definition lists to support communications where needed.
  • Offer cross-department training or briefing to educate one team about what another team does.
Psychological states
  • Other circumstances may affect the audience’s ability to pay attention – operational pressures and deadlines, worries over redundancy or other change.
  • The sender is not in their best psychological state when delivering the message – might be nervous or unprepared.
  • Ensure where appropriate and especially where not directly working with the audience that checks are made as to what is happening and whether there are any current challenges the audience is facing or is aware of. This may be at the forefront of the audience’s minds when the sender starts to transmit the message.
  • Plan and prepare the communication as appropriate in accordance with the need to ensure the best preparation possible.
  • If the sender is busy and needs to compose an important communication they should take time away to get their thoughts together, or take five minutes out to clear their mind.
Lack of clarity about role in communication process
  • Unsure of protocols and processes for making certain communications.
  • Unsure of the role the individual should be taking in a particular work situation.
  • Lack of clear guidance on roles and responsibilities regarding communication.
  • Ensure teams are briefed and trained on the processes and protocols needed to communicate effectively in their roles.
  • Ensure team members know the content of their role and job descriptions and what they should be communicating.
  • Ensure team members know all of the communication channels that are available to them and when to use them.
  • Encourage team members to come up with better options for communications and make suggestions for improvements.
