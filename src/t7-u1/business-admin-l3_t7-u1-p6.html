Managing resources

2 Timescales

When implementing team and organisational plans, managers need to establish timescales for achieving objectives. They need to establish, for example:

  • the deadlines for the team objectives
    aligned with the organisational targets
  • critical review points
    eg at the beginning and end of each stage; when one part of a project cannot start until a previous task has been finished
  • lead times
    eg the time taken to arrange resupply of materials; the notice needed to find, recruit and train more staff
  • the time taken for each small task as part of the overall activity
    eg how long each dish takes to prepare in a restaurant kitchen
  • availability of staff
    eg work schedules allocating enough time for new tasks; holidays and other commitments; time spent on other tasks; necessary training time
  • any restrictions on physical resources
    eg rental periods for specialist equipment; time limits on the use of buildings; noise or emissions limits
  • their own time commitment to each objective or task
    allowing time for each priority, activity, task and objective in the managerâ€™s own work schedule
  • the amount of flexibility in timings
    so that managers can reprioritise and change targets when necessary
  • the time available for unforeseen problems and delays
    eg time allowed in contingency planning for activities that overrun.

Timings need to be structured enough to enable accurate and realistic planning, and flexible enough to cope with problems and unforeseen circumstances.
