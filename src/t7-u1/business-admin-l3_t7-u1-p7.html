Managing resources

4 Physical resources

Physical resources are the things that are needed to be able to implement plans. They can include, for example:

  • buildings and infrastructure — eg factories, workshops, offices, shops; dedicated areas within buildings; hospitals, schools and other community buildings; outdoor work areas, roads and car parks
  • furniture, fixtures and fittings — eg desks, chairs, tables and shelving; display and storage fittings; security systems, and CCTV; lighting, air conditioning and extraction systems
  • equipment and machinery — eg production line machinery and conveyor belts; cleaning equipment; quality control testing equipment, probes and sensors; crockery and glassware in the hospitality trade
  • information technology — eg PCs, laptops and monitors; printers, scanners and copiers; mobile phones, tablets and other devices; software programs and applications; Internet, network and intranet connections
  • vehicles and other transport — eg cars, vans, lorries, forklifts, aircraft and freight trains
  • materials used in production — eg raw materials such as steel or foodstuffs; paper products; fuel; textiles, plastics and chemicals.

Managers need to plan and monitor the physical resources carefully to make sure that:

  • the items and buildings are fit for purpose
  • the resources they need are available
  • the lead time is acceptable – the time taken for reorders to arrive
  • the supply chain is reliable – so that resources can be ordered with confidence
  • they have contingency plans if there are supply problems.
