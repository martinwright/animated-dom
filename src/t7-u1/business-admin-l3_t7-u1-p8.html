Managing resources

5 Human resources

Human resources need to be planned carefully too. Managers need to work out the range of skills, knowledge and experience that they need on their team to be able to achieve the objectives. This is the case for normal additional tasks, such as doing an extra production run at the end of a shift, or for a large-scale project of new work, such as setting up a brand new store, factory or office.

The people that managers need to implement team and organisational plans could be, for example:

  • members of the existing workforce
    eg established employees on the manager’s team; staff from other teams and departments
  • bank staff
    eg trained employees who just work from time to time
  • job agency staff
    eg temporary staff who are called in to cover periods of high output, sickness or holidays
  • new recruits
    eg people taken on to cover specific roles that cannot be filled by the existing workforce
  • consultants
    eg individuals or companies that are contracted to do certain tasks for the organisation, in the long term or short term
  • volunteers
    eg recruited to do a full range of short-term or long-term tasks, especially in community and third sector organisations.

As we know, the team members will have a wide range of skills, knowledge and experience. When planning human resources for tasks, managers need to evaluate these and take into account whether staff:

  • are available, fully trained and ready to be fully operational with no further input
  • need a small amount of training and support for them to be fully effective
  • need complete training and guidance from the manager or others
  • come from a reliable source – eg an agency that they use regularly who know the operational requirements
  • are versatile and flexible – eg able and willing to do a variety of different tasks.
