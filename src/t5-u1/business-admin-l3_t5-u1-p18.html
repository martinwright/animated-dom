Effective teamwork

4 Hersey and Blanchard’s Situational Leadership Model

This model of leadership was developed by Paul Hersey and Kenneth Blanchard during the 1960s and has become a very popular workplace leadership and management model offering us four styles of leadership that the manager can apply in different situations when working with and delegating tasks to their team members.

Much like Tannenbaum and Schmidt, this model provides a context to understand different approaches to delegation depending on the factors of competence and commitment that a team member has towards a task.

The four different situations for delegating work in this model are described as development levels and relate to the team member’s competence and their commitment towards successfully completing a task.

Competence is described as transferable skills and knowledge that can be used to complete a task.

Commitment is described as a mixture of motivation to do the task and the confidence the individual has about doing it.

It is this mixture of competence and commitment that the team member has towards a task that allows the manager to diagnose the situation and apply an appropriate delegation style. The four combinations or task development levels are:

  • Development level 1 (D1)
    what Blanchard calls ‘the enthusiastic beginner’ has high commitment but no or low competence and so whilst new to the task they are enthusiastic about learning to do it.
  • Development level 2 (D2)
    ‘the disillusioned learner’ has gained some competence but is now finding the task harder than they thought it would be. They may become frustrated and lose commitment towards the task.
  • Development level 3 (D3)
    ‘capable but cautious’ means the individual has gained significantly more competence or can do the task, but is struggling with commitment and therefore may lack the confidence and/or motivation to see the task through to the finish.
  • Development level 4 (D4)
    the self-reliant achiever is now both competent and committed to the task and perhaps is seen as someone who is an expert and who can help others through the development levels on the same task. The full task can be delegated to D4.

We can see the journey that a person will take upon carrying out a new task through these development levels. People will be working on different tasks and may be at different levels. For some tasks they might be ‘D1’ – new to but excited, and others at ‘D4’ – willing and fully able to do the task.

This clearly impacts the manager’s ability to delegate work. Team members at D1 will need the work to be clearly defined and require higher levels of close supervision with the decisions still being the responsibility of the manager. At the other end of the spectrum, where a person is at D4 and fully committed and competent, the task can be fully delegated to them. Development levels D2 and D3 represent variations of a stage where some competence exists to do the task but the manager retains some control whilst the person gains the experience and commitment to get to D4.

The leader/manager therefore has to decide, through discussions with the team member, what style to apply once they have diagnosed the development level the person is at for the particular task being undertaken.
